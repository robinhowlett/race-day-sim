# Broadcast Style Guide

## Show Title

**"Edge Call"**

A pre-race analysis broadcast where a host and analyst break down the card using a physics-based velocity model, identify where the crowd is concentrated, and explain specific betting structures that exploit the gaps.

## Production Quality

This is a PRODUCED SHOW — not two guys at a coffee shop. Think: a polished racing broadcast with professional pacing, clear segment structure, and deliberate dramatic arc. The host sounds like someone who has done television — composed, articulate, knows how to set up a moment. The analyst sounds like a professional who presents at conferences — precise, authoritative, but willing to be challenged.

The difference from a typical TV racing show: these two are smarter and more honest than what's on TVG/NYRA. They don't hype. They don't pretend every race is a "great betting opportunity." They treat the audience as peers who can handle complexity, uncertainty, and the honest admission of mistakes.

**Production cues:**
- Clear transitions between segments ("Let's shift to the main event")
- The host controls pacing — knows when to push, when to let the analyst go deep, when to move on
- Energy builds across the show: measured opening → building conviction → the main race → emotional aftermath → reflective close
- NOT casual/sloppy. Informed and sharp, but professional.

## Target Length

15-20 minutes total. Enough time to go deep on 3 races, cover the horizontal, and have a proper post-mortem. The pacing should be:
- Opening/card overview: 2 minutes
- Race 1 (the win): 4-5 minutes
- Race 7 (the main event + aftermath): 6-7 minutes
- Horizontal and lessons: 3-4 minutes
- Close: 1 minute

Races where they pass can be mentioned briefly (one sentence each) but don't need segments. The race they also bet (R6) gets 2 minutes — the miss is acknowledged honestly but doesn't dominate.

## Format

- **HOST** — drives the show. Professional broadcast presence. Sets up each race with context (distance, surface, field size, pool), asks incisive questions, represents the audience's skepticism. NOT a yes-man. When the analyst is wrong, the host says so directly. When the analyst is right, the host gives credit but immediately asks "what's next?" Think: a Bob Costas or Rich Eisen who happens to know racing at an expert level.

- **ANALYST** — the model's voice. Precise, quantitative, but translates everything into racing language. Has conviction when the data supports it and admits openly when it doesn't. Earns credibility through specifics (exact numbers, exact dollar amounts) not through claiming authority. When challenged, responds with evidence, not deflection. Think: a quant who used to be a horseplayer, not a horseplayer who bought software.

## Tone

- Professional but not sterile — this is still entertainment, not a lecture
- Confident and direct — opinions stated clearly, not hedged into meaninglessness
- Honest about uncertainty — "we don't have form data here, so this is a spread-and-survive situation"
- Specific — name horses, name prices, name dollar amounts
- Strategic — always connecting the analysis to THE BET. Every observation leads to "and therefore we do X"
- No tout energy. No "I LOVE this horse." No selling picks. This is analysis, not promotion.
- The host and analyst RESPECT each other but they don't always AGREE — disagreement is productive, not antagonistic

## Language Rules

- No undefined acronyms or model jargon (don't say "adj_v0" or "Benter logit")
- DO say: "his velocity curve shows...", "the deceleration model projects...", "based on his last 5 starts..."
- Express speed differences in concrete terms: "he's been running noticeably faster in recent starts" or "his career data suggests he's the fastest horse in this field early, but he can't sustain it"
- When discussing exotics, explain the STRUCTURE: "we want him on top of the trifecta because that's where the market is thinnest"
- When discussing value: "the model thinks he's a 40% better chance than the odds suggest" or "the public has this horse at 13/1 but his raw ability says he belongs at 6 or 7 to 1"
- When discussing the favorite: frame it as "where is the crowd?" not just "is this horse good or bad?"
- Don't NAME the ITP principles as defined terms ("that's the classic equity-over-survival situation"). Instead DESCRIBE the principle and let it emerge naturally. "Even if we survive that leg, we lose money because the favorite pays nothing" is better than "that's a violation of the equity-over-survival framework."

## Recurring Threads

Arguments that should RECUR across the show (not resolved in one exchange):

1. **The pass debate** — Host challenges "9 passes" early. Analyst defends. Then when the Pick 3 dies on a maiden bomb in R5: the host brings it BACK — "See, if we'd played that wider..." but the analyst counters: "Or we'd have burned more money guessing." Never fully resolved.

2. **Trust the model vs be contrarian** — The model says R7 favorite is "solid." Analyst bets against anyway. When it costs them $292: the host holds him accountable. This tension is the show's emotional core and should be the LAST thing discussed.

3. **Structure vs selection** — "We had the right horses but the wrong structure" in R7. This theme connects R1 (right structure = cashed) to R7 (wrong structure = missed). The contrast should be explicit.

## The Close

Do NOT end neatly. The show ends on the unresolved tension between:
- "We made money today" (+21.7% ROI — the process works)
- "We left $292 on the table" (the biggest payoff of the day, missed)
- "We had the right horses" (the model identified the contenders correctly)
- "But we didn't trust our own model's classification" (bet against a horse we called solid)

That tension is the ongoing question: how much to respect the market vs how much to fight it. No clean answer. The audience should leave thinking about it.

## Segment Structure

### Opening (30 seconds)
- Track, date, conditions
- How many races on the card, how many we're playing, total deployment budget
- Headline: "The main event is Race 7 — 14-horse turf field with a $104,000 trifecta pool"

### Pool Assessment (1 minute)
- Where are the biggest pools?
- Which races have the deepest fields (most combinations = most opportunity for mispricing)?
- Which sequences are available for horizontal play?
- "We're passing 9 of 12 races today — here's why that's the right call"

### Race-by-Race Analysis (2-3 minutes per playable race)
For each race we're betting:

1. **The pace story** — who goes to the front, can they sustain it, who benefits if they can't
2. **The favorite** — strong/moderate/vulnerable? Where is the crowd concentrated?
3. **The value** — which horse does the model say is underpriced, and WHY?
4. **The bet** — exact structure, dollar amounts, what has to happen for each ticket to cash

### Horizontal Strategy (1-2 minutes)
- Which sequence, which pool
- Leg-by-leg: where's the hurdle (narrow, strong conviction), where's the survival spread
- Equity check: would each selection GAIN equity if they won this leg?

### Closing (30 seconds)
- Total deployed: $X of $1,000 bankroll
- Biggest conviction: "If one thing hits today, we want it to be..."
- What we're watching for: "If the pace collapses in R7, our closers are live"

## What the HOST asks

- "Walk me through the pace here — who wants the front and what happens to them?"
- "The favorite is 3/1 — is that fair or is the crowd overpaying?"
- "You mentioned Ben Jacob at 13/1 — what makes you think he belongs?"
- "Why trifecta here instead of just a win bet?"
- "What's the worst case? What kills us today?"
- "You're passing Race 5 — what would it take for you to play a race with no form data?"

## What the ANALYST avoids

- "I guarantee..." (nothing is guaranteed)
- "Everybody should..." (personal opinion, not universal advice)
- "The computer says..." (the model informs, the human decides)
- "It's a lock" / "stone cold" / "mortal lock" (tout language)
- Apologizing for passes — passing IS the edge when there's no opinion
