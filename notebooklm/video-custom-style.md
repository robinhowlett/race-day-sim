# Video Custom Style (paste into "Describe a custom visual style")

Professional sports broadcast aesthetic — think ESPN's pre-game analysis shows or Bloomberg's market coverage applied to horse racing. Dark background with high-contrast data panels. Clean typography. No cartoons, no whimsy, no clip art.

OVERALL LOOK:
Dark slate/navy background with crisp white and accent-color text. Data panels appear as floating cards with subtle depth/shadow. Think: a wall of screens in a trading floor or broadcast studio control room. The visual language says "serious quantitative analysis" not "fun day at the races."

COLOR CODING:
- Green accent: positive outcomes, improving form, overlays, value plays
- Red accent: misses, declining form, underlays, the $292 loss
- Gold/amber accent: the favorite (always highlighted differently from value plays)
- White: primary text and data
- Gray: secondary/context information

TYPOGRAPHY:
- Headlines: bold sans-serif, all caps for race headers ("RACE 7 — MAIN EVENT")
- Data: monospace or tabular figures for numbers (speeds, odds, dollar amounts) so columns align cleanly
- Annotations: slightly smaller, in accent color, with arrows or connection lines pointing to what they reference

DATA VISUALIZATION STYLE:
- Field tables: clean grid layout with alternating row shading. Key columns highlighted. Horses with no data should show empty cells conspicuously — the absence of data IS the visual point.
- Pace diagrams: horizontal timeline showing race distance left-to-right. Horses represented as lines that start high (fast) and slope down (deceleration). Steeper slope = fades faster. Flat/rising = closer profile. Color-code by running style (speed = red, stalker = amber, closer = blue).
- Ticket structures: show as a pyramid/funnel — narrow at the top (1-2 horses, strong conviction), widening at 2nd position (3-4 horses), widest at 3rd position (5-6 horses). This visualizes "tight on top, wide underneath."
- Results reveals: should feel like a scoreboard updating. Each position reveals sequentially with a beat between them. The winning horse gets a gold highlight. Horses that were "ours" get green checkmarks. The trifecta payoff appears last and largest.

SPLIT SCREENS:
For the favorite debate in Race 7, use a literal split — left panel shows "MODEL SAYS: SOLID" evidence (low decay, 70% probability, reliable profile), right panel shows "THE CASE AGAINST" (crowd concentration, mediocre win payoff, value in alternatives). The two sides should feel visually balanced — this is a genuine tension, not a straw man.

EMOTIONAL MOMENTS:
- The R1 trifecta cash: data panel pulses green. The $95.75 number should be prominently displayed with the calculation visible ($0.50 × 191.50).
- The R7 miss: linger. Show the result with "WE HAD HIM" annotations in red appearing one by one next to positions 2, 3, 4. Then the $292.90 figure appears large, with a line through it or grayed out — money left on the table. This should feel like a gut punch visually.
- The 64/1 bomb in the Pick 3: show the horse's name (Runforthewoods) and odds appearing unexpectedly, disrupting the neat structure of the sequence diagram.

TRANSITIONS:
Between segments, brief full-screen title cards: "RACE 1: THE CASE" → "RACE 1: THE RESULT" → "RACE 7: MAIN EVENT" etc. These orient the viewer and create clean breaks between analytical and reveal segments.

FINAL FRAME:
The closing question should appear as text on a dark background — no distracting graphics. Just the contrast:
"R1: Trusted the model → $158"
"R7: Fought the model → missed $292"
Let it sit. The visual simplicity of the final frame should contrast with the data-rich panels that preceded it.

WHAT TO AVOID:
- No stock photos of horses or racetracks — this is about DATA, not atmosphere
- No pie charts or basic bar charts — the data is too specific for generic chart types
- No decorative elements that don't convey information
- No animated transitions that feel "fun" — transitions should feel purposeful and editorial
- No cluttered screens — each panel shows ONE thing clearly, then moves to the next
- No infantilizing the audience — they understand racing terminology, don't explain what a trifecta is
